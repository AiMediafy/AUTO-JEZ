<h2>News: 20 Years of Google Translate – A Bit of History, A Glimpse of the Future</h2><p>Google is celebrating the 20th anniversary of Translate – the iconic tool that began as an AI experiment in 2006, and today supports translation in nearly <strong>250 languages</strong>. The company has shared some fun facts, practical tips, and a few new features worth checking out.</p><h2>What does this mean in practice?</h2><ul><li><strong>Context recognition</strong> – Translate is getting better at handling idioms, specialist jargon, and slang. From my perspective, there’s been significant progress, though <strong>with business, medical, or legal documents I still stick to double verification</strong>.</li><li><strong>Real-time translations</strong> – the Conversation feature (speech-to-text) in the app genuinely allows for meetings and calls in several languages. In practice, this means quickly removing barriers – simple math: if a team has 10 people from 3 countries, you can save <strong>1–2 hours per week/per person</strong> on translating briefs and documentation.</li><li><strong>Powered by AI – large language models</strong> – Translate leverages Google’s generative models (PaLM, Gemini), so the quality of text and voice translations is improving. Key point: it’s not just about translating words, but understanding meaning – at this stage, in most automation, that’s fundamental.</li></ul><h2>Looks impressive, but…</h2><ul><li><strong>Quality depends on data</strong> – translation <em>is</em> improving, but AI still relies on large, open corpora. Are complex, niche topics (e.g., specific law, technology) well served? Well, not always. In every implementation I’ve led, <strong>the biggest challenge was with proprietary data</strong> (e.g., industry-specific glossary).</li><li><strong>Business processes</strong> – AI tools require integration: if your documents are in PDF, manual copy-pasting is a no-go. What do many forget? <strong>The Translate API allows automation of many mini-processes</strong> – such as generating reports in two languages or automatically translating emails. Result: a saving of approx. 15 hrs per week × 4 people × 60 PLN/hr = <strong>~14,000 PLN/month</strong> in larger teams.</li><li><strong>People and skills</strong> – Translate is a tool, not a magic translator. What’s crucial: who and how manages the translation process, whether there’s a “double check” culture – otherwise, risk of errors increases.</li></ul><h2>Summary</h2><p>For many companies, Translate has long been a daily tool – but <strong>on a large scale</strong> it requires a strategy: proprietary data, integration with ERP/Workplace, responsibility for translation quality.
