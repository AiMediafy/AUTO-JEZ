<p><strong>Anthropic has officially added Vas Narasimhan, CEO of Novartis, to the Long-Term Benefit Trust board.</strong> This marks the first time a global pharma leader joins the supervisory body of a top AI developer. <strong>From my perspective, this is one of the more subtle but important signals of a shift in strategy among major AI labs.</strong></p>

<h2>What does this mean in practice?</h2>
<p>The Long-Term Benefit Trust (LTBT) is a specific supervisory structure at Anthropic. It aims to balance public interest with commercial viability. Bringing the head of Novartis (a pharmaceutical company with $55B in annual revenue) on board potentially serves several purposes:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Pharmaceuticals and biotechnology</strong> are natural domains for AI—from drug discovery to managing clinical trials.</li>
  <li>The sector is under heavy regulatory pressure and demands long-term thinking—an approach that is increasingly influencing AI strategy after a series of global debates about model safety.</li>
  <li>The LTBT board is ultimately meant to shape the development directions of models and their communication with the market. Someone from Novartis’s management brings practical experience in implementing innovation under strict oversight.</li>
</ul>
<p>From my perspective, I see three real opportunities for Anthropic here:</p>
<ul>
  <li>A more mindful approach to AI risk—looking broadly not just through a technological lens, but also public health, ethics, and sensitive data.</li>
  <li>The ability to build better models tailored to the specific needs of medicine and research (the potential value here is already in the billions of dollars—for example, AI can now accelerate clinical trial phases by 20-30%, i.e. by 2–3 years for selected pathways).</li>
  <li>Additional legitimization for the company when talking to regulators and major clients in pharma/biotech.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Challenges? Specific and substantial</h2>
<p><strong>Having people on the board is not enough—data and integrations are crucial.</strong> In today’s AI deployments for pharma, the main bottlenecks are:</p>
<ul>
  <li>The quality and access to clinical data—often fragmented and highly protected.</li>
  <li>Regulatory approvals and compliance (HIPAA, GDPR, FDA—sometimes these processes take months).</li>
  <li>A workforce ready for transformation—in the medical sector, change is a marathon, not a sprint.</li>
</ul>
<p>Deployments I have led in related sectors show that without business-side partnership, such projects can stall at the pilot stage.</p>

<h2>What are the implications?</h2>
<p>Anthropic wants to be not just a “safer OpenAI” but also a partner for large, cautious sectors like pharma. This is a long-term move, offering a chance for real impact on public health <strong>(but without illusions—it requires investment in data, processes and people’s education)</strong>. If other companies follow this path, AI could genuinely accelerate innovation in healthcare.</p>
