<p><strong>Anthropic and NEC have just announced a partnership aimed at building the largest AI engineering team in Japan.</strong> This is not the first time a major IT producer has attempted to translate global AI trends to the local market, but the scale of this project is decidedly ambitious. </p> <h2>What does this mean in practice?</h2> <ul> <li><strong>Training and hiring thousands of AI engineers</strong> – NEC wants to build up its ML capabilities practically from scratch, leveraging Anthropic technology (Claude, model building tools, and AI safety).</li> <li><strong>Enterprise orders for generative AI</strong> – the main focus: process automation, chatbots, and customer support for Japan’s largest corporations, government, and service sectors. A typically Japanese specialization – not an experiment, but production.</li> <li><strong>Technology localization</strong> – crucial: emphasis on Japanese language support and compliance with local GDPR/company secrets requirements. From my perspective, this is exactly where they have the edge over “off-the-shelf” Western solutions.</li> </ul> <h2>Real business and challenges</h2> <ul> <li><strong>Costs vs. actual value:</strong> Preliminary estimates speak of several hundred million PLN annually (converted from the overall scale of the AI market in Japan). However, it’s mainly about <strong>time and know-how</strong>. For example: automating customer service in a large Japanese retail chain – traditionally about 80 people × 160 h/month × 80 PLN/h = nearly 1 million PLN monthly labor costs. AI can realistically reduce this amount by 20-30% and shift employees to more complex tasks.</li> <li><strong>Scaling processes:</strong> The biggest challenge: availability of people who understand the local language, culture, and data specifics (e.g., Japanese forms, legacy ERP systems, public sector). In the West, this stage often fails – here there’s a chance it will be different if NEC trains people effectively.</li> <li><strong>Risk of diluted competence:</strong> Many training initiatives have ended up with dozens of “half-baked” projects. The key is linking training directly to concrete implementations – without this, an AI team without a data and process analysis backbone will not build an advantage.</li> </ul> <h2>Summary + implementer’s perspective</h2> <p>In my experience: these types of partnerships make sense when <strong>training + infrastructure + concrete projects</strong> go hand in hand. In Japan, the cultural and language barrier is bigger than it seems, but if Anthropic delivers on the technology and NEC truly overhauls the processes, this move could set them up for years. It’s worth watching to see if the first large-scale implementations appear in e-commerce and the public sector – that’s where it will be clear if this investment delivers an edge, or ends up as slideware and in reports.</p> <p>Let me know in the comments what you think about this approach to building AI teams.
