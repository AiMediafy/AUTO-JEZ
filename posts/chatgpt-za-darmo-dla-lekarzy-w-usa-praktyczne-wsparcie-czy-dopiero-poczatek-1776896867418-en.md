<p><strong>OpenAI announced that ChatGPT will now be free for verified doctors, nurses, and pharmacists in the USA.</strong> The goal: support in clinical care, documentation, and research. A practical step—especially as pressure for efficiency and cost reduction in healthcare continues to grow.</p>

<h2>What does this mean in practice?</h2>
<ul>
  <li>Specifically: free access to ChatGPT (for now, web version), after professional credentials are verified.</li>
  <li>Areas of support: drafting medical documentation, preparing visit summaries, searching for scientific information, a “second opinion” for unusual clinical cases.</li>
  <li>Direct integration with hospital systems is not yet available—this is a standalone application, not an EHR plugin.</li>
</ul>
<p>From my perspective, two key aspects stand out:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Efficiency –</strong> estimates from deployments (mainly in the USA) show that automatic support for medical documentation can reduce time spent with the system by up to 30–60 minutes per day per person. For four doctors in a clinic, this gives: <strong>0.5 h x 20 days x 4 people x 250 PLN/h ≈ 10,000 PLN monthly</strong>. Real savings.</li>
  <li><strong>Safety and responsibility –</strong> ChatGPT does not make diagnoses or take over clinical decisions. It’s an auxiliary tool—the doctor is ultimately responsible for the outcome. Therefore, every suggestion must be verified. At this stage, “full automation” is out of the question.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Opportunities and challenges</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Opportunity:</strong> Slight lowering of the entry barrier—no costs, quick start for those interested.</li>
  <li><strong>Limitations:</strong> The biggest challenge is integration with the hospital workflow—copying/pasting takes time, and a complete implementation requires a “bridge” between AI and hospital systems (EHR, HIS). Without this, efficiency gains are limited.</li>
  <li><strong>Data and privacy:</strong> The ChatGPT version for clinicians meets HIPAA requirements, but in practice, security policies and staff training procedures must be implemented. Not everyone knows how to anonymize data before feeding it to AI.</li>
  <li><strong>Sterile English:</strong> This solution is currently only for the American market; it’s unclear when it will appear elsewhere—also due to legal complexities.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Conclusions</h2>
<p>Making ChatGPT available to clinicians free of charge is a <strong>step in a real direction</strong>, although the real revolution will begin once AI is directly integrated into the hospital ecosystem. In practice, deployment procedures (training, security, integrations) will be key.</p>
