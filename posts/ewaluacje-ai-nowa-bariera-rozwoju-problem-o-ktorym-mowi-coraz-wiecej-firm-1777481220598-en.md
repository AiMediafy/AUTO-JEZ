<p><strong>The rise in AI performance is no longer just about supercomputers or ever-larger models. A new brake is becoming increasingly apparent—the process of <em>evaluation</em>. It's getting ever costlier, ever harder to scale. This is the focus of a new report from Hugging Face, which is resonating strongly in the industry—including in the context of tools developed by OpenAI or Anthropic.</strong></p> <h2>What Is Changing in AI Evaluation?</h2> <ul> <li>Models are growing, and with them, the cost and complexity of testing them. Example: evaluating LLMs is no longer a few hours on a cluster, but hundreds or thousands of hours of computational and human work.</li> <li>The key here is: running the model is one thing, but rigorous validation (without heuristics, "quick fixes," and messy prompts) is becoming a separate, substantial stage in the implementation process.</li> <li>From my perspective—more and more deployments are stumbling at this stage. Companies spend budgets on GPUs, but then lack resources/time/data for real <strong>quality tests</strong> that make a difference.</li> </ul> <h2>How Much Does It Cost? Simple Math</h2> <ul> <li>An example from the report: testing 1,000 new prompt variants requires several hundred person-hours (let’s assume 200 hours).</li> <li>If four people work for a week (4 × 40 h × 70 PLN/h), we're talking about <strong>over 11,000 PLN just for personnel</strong>, plus a few thousand PLN for infrastructure.</li> <li>Scaling to large systems—monthly evaluation costs begin to exceed the cost of training smaller models themselves.</li> </ul> <h2>Are Companies Ready?</h2> <p>In practice, this means:</p> <ul> <li>There is a need for <strong>good reference datasets</strong> (which is the most demanding part!),</li> <li>Evaluation processes must be <strong>automated</strong> and well thought out, for example using proprietary tools or open frameworks (there’s a lot going on at Hugging Face, but few companies in Poland make use of this!),</li> <li>People with QA experience in AI are starting to be as valuable as model experts.</li> </ul> <p>I have seen many deployments where “everything fell apart” at the testing stage—because this cost was overlooked or addressed too late. <strong>Without sensible evaluation, it’s hard to prove ROI from AI investments.</strong></p> <h2>Summary</h2> <p>If you are serious about implementing AI, put a question mark not only next to architecture and data, but also next to the testing process. It's easy to lose control and your budget here. An open question for the industry—if you want to talk about how to optimize AI evaluation in practice, feel free to comment or send a private message.</p>
