<p><strong>Today's news:</strong> James Manyika from Google talks with LL COOL J about AI and creativity in the latest episode of the Dialogues on Technology and Society series. My quick take – it’s interesting how major companies are more and more actively promoting the topic of creativity in the context of AI, but at the same time, there’s a lack of concrete business takeaways.</p>

<h2>What does this mean in practice?</h2>
<ul>
  <li>The conversation focuses mainly on AI’s potential to support new forms of expression – creating music, generating images, writing text.</li>
  <li>The role of AI as a tool for creators was highlighted, not just as a “machine for automation.”</li>
  <li>From a technical perspective: Google is vigorously developing generative models – Gemini, MusicLM, Imagen. In the context of this discussion, it’s especially about democratizing creative opportunities (greater access for people without classic artistic skills).</li>
  <li><strong>The challenges</strong> include the quality of input data (for example, in music generation – licenses, copyrights, ethics), scalability, and real integration into creators’ workflows.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Business value: where does this already work today?</h2>
<ul>
  <li>Implementation example: marketing teams are using text and image generators, saving several hours a week on preparing materials.</li>
  <li>Real numbers: for a 4-person team, savings amount to about 10–15h per week × 4 people × 60 PLN/h = approx. 10-12 thousand PLN monthly (assuming the tools are integrated into a continuous process). The potential grows with automation – generating storyboards, campaign proposals, slogan variants.</li>
  <li>From my perspective, it’s crucial that creative AI implementations aren’t a “plaything for the chosen few.” Processes need to be automated, well-documented, and safeguarded in terms of copyright.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Conclusions – what to do today?</h2>
<ul>
  <li>Don’t wait for the next “great revolution” – start testing available generative tools now.</li>
  <li>Copyright and legal issues should be a constant element of your implementation strategy.</li>
  <li>The key is integrating AI into daily workflows – not just for inspiration, but for real tasks. In my projects, the best results came from connecting AI into platforms the team is already using.</li>
</ul>
