<p><strong>News of the day: Google is launching three new security and compliance features for Google Ads users, available within the Ads Advisor tool.</strong> From my perspective: this is a response to real market needs and the increasing scale of errors/risks in campaign automation. The question: will it solve the daily pains of specialists?</p>

<h2>What is Google introducing?</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Advertising Policy Compliance Verification</strong> (Policy Guard): automatic checking of whether creatives and campaign settings violate Google's guidelines—before they are published.</li>
  <li><strong>Data Security Violation Detection</strong> (Safety Sentinel): the system analyzes whether ads potentially expose the organization to data leaks or phishing, based on AI.</li>
  <li><strong>Consent Monitoring</strong> (Consent Checker): checking whether collected user data has appropriate consents—in accordance with local regulations.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>The key aspect:</strong> everything operates semi-automatically within a single dashboard. The number of cases where a campaign is suspended or blocked due to an error should noticeably drop.</p>

<h2>What does this actually mean for companies?</h2>
<ul>
  <li>Fewer campaigns <strong>blocked "after the fact"</strong> (during moderation or due to user complaints)—which shortens reaction times by up to 2–3 days per week.</li>
  <li>Fewer <strong>unplanned advertising pauses</strong>: with a budget of PLN 15,000/month and daily leads worth around PLN 150—a single blocked day = a real loss of approx. PLN 1,000.</li>
  <li><strong>Greater transparency</strong>: SEM and marketing specialists get quick feedback on <em>what and why</em> might be blocking a campaign, without having to constantly "poke" Google Support.</li>
  <li><strong>Easier compliance reporting</strong>: smaller companies avoid unintentional errors (e.g., missing consent checkboxes), which still commonly happen in the industry.</li>
</ul>

<p>Of course, AI "guards" will not fully replace vigilant teams and robust process documentation. But in practice, I have seen implementations where even 20–25% of appeals were due to unintuitive blocks—here, such tools can save several hours of work per campaign each week.</p>

<h2>What remains to be done?</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Data and processes</strong>: Up-to-date checklists, training on Google's policies, and a well-designed review process—automated tools won't detect everything, and the speed of response still depends on people.</li>
  <li><strong>Integration with your own systems</strong>: If you have your own reporting tools—check how these features will fit into your workflow.</li>
</ul>

<p>In summary: <strong>this is a step toward truly relieving marketers and improving ad results (fewer blocks = more conversions = lower opportunity costs).</strong> But without solid data + processes, we still can't avoid mistakes.</p>
