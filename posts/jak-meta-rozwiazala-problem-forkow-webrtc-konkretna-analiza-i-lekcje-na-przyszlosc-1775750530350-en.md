<p><strong>Meta has published a behind-the-scenes look at the modernization of its implementation of WebRTC – the real-time audio and video technology used in applications like Messenger, Instagram, and Workplace. From an AI architect’s perspective: this news is significant because it shows how a giant company deals with a typical problem – becoming separated from the main development of an open-source tool.</strong></p><h2>What actually happened?</h2><ul><li>Over the years, Meta developed its own "fork" of WebRTC (that is, a separate version in its monorepo). The reason? Adapting it to meet internal needs, security, scalability, and integration with hundreds of microservices.</li><li>The typical fork problem: over time, you lag behind on updates, bug fixes, and new features developed by the community.</li><li>Meta performed a technical "escape from the fork trap" – by implementing a dual-stack architecture. In practice: their system can now use both the modified version and the main "community" version of WebRTC.</li><li>The migration process covered over <strong>50 different use cases</strong> (applications, tools, platforms), hundreds of engineers, and hundreds of thousands of users of these tools every day.</li></ul><h2>Technical takeaway – what does this give?</h2><ul><li><strong>Flexibility:</strong> It’s possible to implement new features and fixes from the main repository more quickly, without getting blocked by internal modifications.</li><li><strong>Savings:</strong> No more budget spent on "catching up" with upstream – it can be assumed that this means <strong>thousands of hours of work</strong> per year, which roughly (50 engineers × 100 h/year × 120 PLN/h = about 600,000 PLN/year) – a real value.</li><li><strong>Security:</strong> Faster reaction to critical bugs and vulnerabilities – fewer gaps, less stress.</li><li><strong>Better collaboration with open source:</strong> The ability to share some changes and actively influence tool development.</li></ul><h2>Challenges in such a migration</h2><ul><li><strong>Data and API synchronization:</strong> In the deployments I’ve led, it's crucial to manage discrepancies between versions – tests, monitoring, rollback.</li><li><strong>People:</strong> Engineers need to learn new processes – it pays to invest in training and documentation.</li><li><strong>DevOps processes:</strong> Without automation and CI/CD, nothing will move forward. Fast response to upstream changes is key here.</li></ul><p><strong>Conclusions:</strong> Meta approached open source very pragmatically – in practice, this proves that forking large systems must be "agile," otherwise we risk stagnation and high costs. It’s worth learning from their story and planning migration today – not waiting until the "fork" is outdated.</p><p>Do you have a similar problem, planning your own open-source projects, want to "unblock" your architecture?
