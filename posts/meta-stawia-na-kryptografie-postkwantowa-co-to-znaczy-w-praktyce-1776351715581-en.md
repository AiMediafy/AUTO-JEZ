<h2>Meta reveals details of its migration to post-quantum cryptography</h2><p>Post-quantum cryptography (PQC) is a topic gaining increasing attention in the industry. Meta has just shared its experiences in deploying PQC within key systems. From my perspective, it's not only an important step for data security – but also a signal that it's time for practical preparations for the new era of information protection.</p><h2>What new solutions is Meta proposing?</h2><ul><li><strong>Framework and migration levels for PQC:</strong> Meta is not only implementing new algorithms, but also publishing a framework approach (so-called "PQC Migration Levels") – any organization can assess its level of readiness and plan its own migration path.</li><li><strong>Lessons learned:</strong> The company shares what worked and where "bottlenecks" emerged. They clearly emphasize that the biggest problem is system compatibility and managing updates on a global scale.</li><li><strong>Practical guide:</strong> Concrete stages are described: from infrastructure audit, through PQC performance testing, to implementation and monitoring the impact of changes.</li></ul><h2>Why is this important and what does it mean for business?</h2><p>In practice, the transition to post-quantum cryptography means:</p><ul><li><strong>Security for decades ahead</strong> – traditional algorithms like RSA will, in a few years, be vulnerable to attacks using quantum computers. PQC offers a chance to avoid costly incidents.</li><li><strong>Real migration costs:</strong> It is estimated that an organization with 500 systems integrating data may need as much as <strong>15–18 thousand man-hours for analysis and implementation</strong> (with a 6-person team, that's a year of work). The cost? About <strong>1.2–1.5 million PLN</strong> just for labor time. That's not including tools, training, and testing.</li><li><strong>Key challenges:</strong> Data that can no longer be "disconnected" from old protocols; the infrastructure required for testing and safe rollout; and, of course, the competence of developer teams.</li></ul><h2>From my observations</h2><p>In the projects I run, the biggest problem is the "invisible connections" between systems – often undocumented, but crucial for data exchange. PQC migration highlights the greatest risks right here.</p><p><strong>It's worth conducting a dependency audit today and appointing "PQC champions" – both on the IT and business side.</strong> This is not a one-quarter project, but a multi-year programme of changes with real impact on IT risk and company value.</p><h2>Summary</h2><p>PQC is not just a trend – it's a requirement of the coming decade. Meta has set a practical standard, but each company must define its own path. If you'd like to discuss this for your organization – write to me. Or let me know in the comments how this looks at your company.</p>
