<p><strong>OpenAI has announced a new collaboration with the Gates Foundation – the goal is to support Asian crisis response teams in the practical use of AI.</strong> Straight away: the initiative targets real problems faced by emergency services, not just a PR showcase of technology. From my perspective – this is the right direction, though the teams have a lot of work ahead of them.</p>
<h2>What is this action about?</h2>
<ul>
  <li>Workshops and pilot deployments of AI tools (mainly LLMs from OpenAI) for organizations responding to natural disasters in Asia.</li>
  <li>Partnership with the Gates Foundation means real funding + hands-on knowledge from working with NGOs.</li>
  <li>Main tasks: rapid data analysis, multilingual translations, alert generation, automation of reports and requests from local communities.</li>
</ul>
<h2>What does this change in practice?</h2>
<p>The biggest advantage: <strong>reduction of response time and better information analysis</strong>. A numerical example – a typical report/alert to services (e.g. via email or SMS) analyzed by a human takes 5–10 minutes. By automating 200 reports per day (the average for post-disaster areas), teams can gain back <strong>over 16 hours of work per day</strong> (200 × 5 min = 1000 min ≈ 16 h) – at a rate of 70 PLN/hour, that’s over <strong>1100 PLN saved daily</strong>, which is about 33,000 PLN monthly. And these are very conservative estimates.</p>
<p>AI also helps overcome language barriers (hundreds of dialects and varieties in Asia), which <strong>significantly speeds up communication with local communities</strong>. The key is that administration doesn’t waste time on translations and information selection, they just take action.</p>
<h2>Main challenges and what needs to be done</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>High-quality data:</strong> AI is only as useful as the input data. With poorly structured reports (SMS, emails, contacts from people unfamiliar with technology) – implementation requires proper templating and training for staff.</li>
  <li><strong>Processes and integration:</strong> An AI model is effective when it’s integrated with the service teams’ existing communication channels. In practice, an IT project like this is about adapting AI to the actual workflow (not the other way around).</li>
  <li><strong>Security and ethics:</strong> Processing large volumes of sensitive data (victims, locations, phone numbers) requires transparency, access control, and language model monitoring (data leaks are still possible – and not just theoretically).</li>
</ul>
<p>From my experience – in pilots, key factors were the engagement of local teams, real-world testing, and ongoing validation of outcomes by people in the field.</p>
<h2>Summary</h2>
<p>The move by OpenAI and the Gates Foundation is a chance for a noticeable change in the speed and effectiveness of aid in Asian countries. The potential: literally thousands of man-hours recovered monthly, more effective communication, and better access to support. Challenges? Of course – here the <strong>focus on data, processes, and collaboration with local teams</strong> is essential, not just the technology.</p>
