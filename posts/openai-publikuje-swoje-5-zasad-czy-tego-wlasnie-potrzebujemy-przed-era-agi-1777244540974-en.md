<p><strong>OpenAI has published its '5 principles', meant to be a compass during the building and implementation of AGI.</strong> The idea is good—because discussions of ethics and AI's impact on people can no longer be avoided. But do these principles actually solve real challenges?</p>

<h2>What's it about?</h2>
<p>On the <a href="https://openai.com/index/our-principles">OpenAI</a> website, the company officially presented five principles they plan to follow in their work on Artificial General Intelligence (AGI):</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Benefit for all humanity.</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Long-term safety.</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Decisions made through broad social dialogue.</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Openness to partnerships and cooperation.</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Realistic approach to potential and risks (acceptance of uncertainty and readiness to adapt).</strong></li>
</ul>

<p><strong>From my perspective</strong>: it's a very general declaration, but an important one—in practice, it's the boundary between 'acting technologically' and 'taking responsibility for what we do.' I've already seen similar announcements in previous years, but such a broad document in the midst of AI development <em>matters</em>.</p>

<h2>What does this mean in practice?</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Social consultations</strong> – OpenAI wants to discuss technical and ethical dilemmas more widely. A signal for enterprises: there’s a growing expectation for transparency and auditability of algorithms.</li>
  <li><strong>Taking safety seriously</strong> – this isn’t just PR statements. In large deployments that I run, actively managing risk (from data protection to clear rules for using AI models) is increasingly crucial.</li>
  <li><strong>Partnerships and ecosystem</strong> – AI doesn’t develop in isolation. In practice: if you are getting deeper into AI in your company, look for solutions that can be integrated and co-created with others.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Specifics for business</h2>
<p><strong>Why do you need these principles?</strong> Today, AI implementations (automation of document workflows, customer service, data analysis) without clear principles will end up clashing with compliance, PR risks, or IT blockages. Example: instead of a 'quick saving of 10,000 PLN monthly on automation,' you get a process nobody wants to use — because management prefers not to risk reputationally.</p>

<p>From my experience: transparent guidelines (not just declarations) shorten implementation negotiations by even several days. Simple math: <strong>2 weeks shorter implementation × 3 people involved × 60 PLN/h = around 14,000 PLN in real savings</strong>.</p>

<h2>To sum up</h2>
<p><strong>The direction is good—but execution matters</strong>. OpenAI sets the bar again: it’s not enough to have 'AI in the company.' What’s needed are <strong>concrete policies, continuous team education, and adaptive AI audit processes</strong>.</p>
