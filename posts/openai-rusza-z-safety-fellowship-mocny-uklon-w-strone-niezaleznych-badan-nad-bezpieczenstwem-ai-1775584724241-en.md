<h2>OpenAI Safety Fellowship – a fresh initiative with potentially big impact</h2><p>OpenAI announced today the launch of the <a href="https://openai.com/index/introducing-openai-safety-fellowship" target="_blank">Safety Fellowship</a> – a pilot program designed to support independent researchers and practitioners in the fields of safety and alignment in AI. From my perspective, this is a response to growing pressure from the academic and business communities to further invest in the safe development of advanced models.</p><h2>What exactly is OpenAI proposing?</h2><ul><li><strong>Top young researchers and practitioners</strong> will get the chance to conduct their own projects related to AI safety – outside the framework of large labs.</li><li><strong>Funding + mentoring</strong> – OpenAI will cover fellowships and provide expert support. The goal is to create an environment for talent development, not just another recruitment channel.</li><li><strong>Pilot means small scale at first</strong> – the first, several-month cycles are planned for 2026.</li></ul><p><strong>In practice, this means:</strong> OpenAI is opening up to external ideas and supporting diverse approaches to AI safety, also outside its core structure. This is a direction strongly advocated by AI Safety experts from organizations like Anthropic and non-profits, who argue that one lab alone cannot tackle the issue on its own.</p><h2>Opportunities and challenges for business</h2><ul><li><strong>Increased transparency and innovation</strong> – if research findings are made public, the whole industry gains new tools and safety standards.</li><li><strong>Growth of expertise in the market</strong> – more independent experts = more support for companies implementing AI; this is a real opportunity to fill knowledge gaps inside organizations.</li><li><strong>The question of real impact</strong> – the program is small; what's crucial here isn't just the funding, but the support structure and the scope of published results.</li></ul><p>In my own advanced AI deployments, I see how <strong>safety topics/alignment problems are starting to take center stage</strong>, also among Polish clients. In short: without solid tools and standards being developed also outside of closed labs, progress will be slow (and at times, risky).</p><h2>What does this mean?</h2><p>OpenAI's move signals that big players are starting to take seriously the arguments for <strong>openness and pluralism in approaches to AI safety</strong>. Will this solve the trust issues around AI implementation? Not overnight. But it’s a step in the right direction, one that—if followed by real publications and collaboration—will unlock new possibilities (and more peace of mind for technical and business decision-makers).</p><p>If you want to discuss how to approach AI safety in your company or you’re interested in this program – message me privately.</p>
