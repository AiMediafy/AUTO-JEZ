<p><strong>OpenAI has just announced the expansion of its Trusted Access for Cyber program. Selected partners are gaining access to a new model—GPT-5.4-Cyber, and at the same time, the company is raising the bar regarding security and access controls. From my perspective, this is a logical step: AI is increasingly entering the realm of cybersecurity, and a responsible approach to the dissemination of tools is essential here.</strong></p>

<h2>What has actually been announced?</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Expansion of the Trusted Access for Cyber program</strong> – more companies and organizations will receive access to advanced AI-supported cyber defense features.</li>
  <li><strong>GPT-5.4-Cyber</strong> – a new model designed specifically for threat detection, incident analysis, and supporting SOC (Security Operations Center).</li>
  <li><strong>Strengthening of control mechanisms</strong> – OpenAI is focusing on precise recipient verification and new safeguards to reduce the risk of abuse.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>In practice, this means:</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Better tailored AI tools for 'cyber defenders'—that is, companies that actively ensure the security of IT infrastructure.</li>
  <li>A clear signal that advanced models (e.g., for log analysis automation or threat hunting) will not be widely available 'on demand' to just anyone interested.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Opportunities and challenges</h2>
<p>From my experience, <strong>AI in cybersecurity can reduce SOC analysts' labor costs by as much as 30–40%</strong>—especially in areas with repetitive alert analysis and correlation of millions of records a day. To put that into numbers: if a four-person SOC team saves about 15 hours weekly × 4 people × 80 PLN/hour, that's savings in the range of <strong>19–20 thousand PLN per month</strong> on a single process.</p>
<p>At the same time (and this is key), the effectiveness of such implementations depends on data quality, good processes, and regular team training. An ‘out of the box’ AI model will never recognize the nuances of a specific environment without this implementation work.</p>
<p><strong>The second challenge:</strong> proper permissions. Too broad access to such tools could be exploited by people with malicious intent. That's why I consider OpenAI's move towards selective provisioning to be reasonable. I've seen similar attempts at opening up tools in previous years—and they always ended up risking leaks or misuse.</p>

<h2>Conclusions and recommendations</h2>
<p>To sum up: <strong>AI automating cyber defense is not a 'nice to have', but increasingly a necessity</strong>. If you’re considering such deployments, start by organizing your processes, setting access policies, and auditing data—before reaching for advanced models like GPT-5.4-Cyber.</p>
