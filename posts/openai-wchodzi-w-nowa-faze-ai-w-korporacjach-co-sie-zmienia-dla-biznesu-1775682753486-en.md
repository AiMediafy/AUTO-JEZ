<h2>The next phase of enterprise AI – OpenAI</h2><p>Today, OpenAI published a roadmap for the next stage of AI deployments in companies: <strong>Frontier, ChatGPT Enterprise, Codex</strong>, and so-called company-wide AI agents. From my perspective – this announcement clearly shows that AI is entering the stage of mass business operationalization. It’s worth taking a closer look.</p><h2>What is actually changing?</h2> <ul> <li><strong>Frontier</strong> – this is a new line of models, developed for companies that need “more, faster, safer.” It is designed to support crucial processes, such as data analysis, document automation, and code generation.</li> <li><strong>ChatGPT Enterprise</strong> – a platform that is already being implemented in large organizations. It is getting new features: personalization, integration with transactional systems, better data and workflow control.</li> <li><strong>Codex</strong> – increasingly close to production use. It automates programming, enables teams to quickly build tools – this has resulted, for example, in shortening development cycles by 30–40% in the projects I’ve seen.</li> <li><strong>Company-wide AI agents</strong> – this is the most important change. Instead of individual chatbots, each employee can have “their own agent” connected to processes, tickets, documentation, workflow. In practice – genuine automation of repetitive tasks.</li> </ul> <h2>Challenges and opportunities – what needs to be done for this to work?</h2> <ul> <li><strong>Data:</strong> Without organized data, integrations, and security policies, the agent won’t be useful. The key here is preparing for data migration and API harmonization.</li> <li><strong>Processes:</strong> Automation pays off if you map real workflows (not just “a chatbot to solve simple questions”). You need to build a backend that allows for rapid iterations.</li> <li><strong>People:</strong> From my perspective, the greatest resistance is around changing habits and adapting. OpenAI sees this clearly: they focus on UX, onboarding, and customizing agents for different roles. That makes sense, but it requires inside-out work.</li> <li><strong>Infrastructure:</strong> When I speak with clients, the most common challenges are cost control, model availability (SLA), and cost prediction at large scale.</li> </ul> <h2>Real numbers – where is the return on investment?</h2> <ul> <li><strong>Automation of repetitive tasks:</strong> Even in a mid-sized company: 20 people × 2 hours a day × 60 PLN/h = about 24,000 PLN a month to be recovered.</li> <li><strong>Coding automation with Codex:</strong> 30% reduction in development time – time is money.</li> <li><strong>It’s not just about savings</strong> – real advantages: faster rollouts, better customer support, fewer errors.</li> </ul> <h2>My perspective</h2> <p>Looking at AI deployments over the past 3–4 years: the key is smart adaptation of these tools to actual business goals. OpenAI is moving toward “AI as a digital worker” – much closer to what companies really need. But implementation requires more than just a license – it’s necessary to reorganize processes, invest in data and training.</p><p>Let me know in the comments if you’re planning a large-scale AI implementation – I’d be happy to share my experience.
