<h2>OpenAI releases GPT-5.4 mini and nano – a concrete change for software teams</h2><p>Today, OpenAI announced <a href="https://openai.com/index/introducing-gpt-5-4-mini-and-nano">two new models: GPT-5.4 mini and nano</a>. Both are smaller, faster, and more efficient versions of GPT-5.4 – dedicated, among others, to coding, working with tools, multimodal reasoning, and handling large API loads and sub-agents. From my perspective: this is a very pragmatic upgrade. Such models often make the difference when it comes to practical AI deployment.</p><h2>What does this mean in practice?</h2><ul><li><strong>Coding and integrations:</strong> Mini and nano are optimized for error detection, code generation, and automation of simple tasks (<em>importantly – faster and cheaper</em>, at the cost of a slight decrease in generality).</li><li><strong>API & sub-agents:</strong> Mini/nano are designed to handle a very large number of parallel queries – crucial when building AI that needs to operate at scale (e.g., thousands of agents performing tasks simultaneously).</li><li><strong>Multimodality:</strong> They support processing text, images, and code snippets in one place – which can be leveraged in devops tools, ticketing systems, documentation generation, and more.</li><li><strong>Lower costs and reduced latency:</strong> OpenAI promises cost reductions of up to 40% and shorter response times, especially when working with the API.</li></ul><h2>What does this bring to business?</h2><ul><li><strong>Personal calculations:</strong> If an entire team of developers (e.g., 4 people) uses GPT for generating tests, refactoring, or bug analysis – previous LLMs provided savings of around 15 hrs per week × 4 people × 60 PLN/hr = approx. 14–15 thousand PLN monthly. Mini/nano offer a chance to <strong>reduce this cost by an additional 20–30%</strong> through faster handling and a lower API price tag.</li><li><strong>Better scalability:</strong> If you build AI products "for clients," sudden spikes in traffic won't be as problematic.</li><li><strong>Realistic requirements:</strong> I've already seen similar announcements in previous years, and key factors were: clean data, well-documented processes, and a team aware that not every automation works from day one.</li></ul><h2>Challenges?</h2><ul><li><strong>Model selection:</strong> It’s essential to test clearly: do mini/nano lose any crucial features compared to the larger GPT-5.4? For very complex tasks – sometimes a bigger model proves more precise.</li><li><strong>Integration:</strong> When migrating large systems to a new API – it’s worth allowing extra time for functional testing.</li></ul><h2>Conclusions</h2><p>This is not a revolution, but a concrete step forward for teams building their own AI tools. Smaller models mean real savings and faster development – provided they are well-configured. <strong>If you’re building AI for a software house, workflow tools, or working on your own agents:</strong> it’s definitely worth taking a look at mini/nano. Let me know in the comments if you’d like to discuss an implementation – subscribe if you want more analyses like this.</p>
